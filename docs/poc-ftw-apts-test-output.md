### L0 — Access Probability Engine (APE)

<div align="center">

![APE Score](https://img.shields.io/badge/APE_Score-38_%2F_100-orange?style=flat-square)
![Cascade](https://img.shields.io/badge/Cascade-ACTIVE_(APE_%3C_50)-red?style=flat-square)
![Ceiling Adj](https://img.shields.io/badge/Ceiling_Adjustment-×0.85_Applied-orange?style=flat-square)

</div>

**What this layer does:** APE calculates the probability that you can actually get access to this job before it's locked up. It weighs four factors: existing relationships with decision-makers, permit visibility, whether the deal is off-market, and gatekeeper complexity.

**What it found here:** This is a **closed-loop public housing redevelopment**. Fort Worth Housing Solutions is a mission-driven nonprofit and the property owner. Ojala Partners LP is their development partner. Neither is an absentee or distressed seller — they are organized, funded, and connected. There is no auction, no broker, no open bidding process. Access requires either a prior relationship or a proactive cold contact strategy.

**Why it matters:** A low APE score (38/100) doesn't mean pass — it means **adjust your bid ceiling downward** and **change your play type**. The 0.85 cascade multiplier was automatically applied to the bid ceiling in L7, protecting you from overbidding on a deal where access probability is constrained. APE below 50 always triggers the cascade in SIE v1.7.

### L1A — TDLR Watch Protocol *(NEW — v1.7)*

<div align="center">

![Watch Flag](https://img.shields.io/badge/Watch_Flag-ACTIVE-red?style=for-the-badge&logo=radar&logoColor=white)
![Status](https://img.shields.io/badge/TABS_Status-PENDING_—_No_Filing_Found-orange?style=flat-square)
![GC](https://img.shields.io/badge/GC_Identified-NO_—_Window_Open-critical?style=flat-square)
![Action](https://img.shields.io/badge/Action-Search_Weekly-yellow?style=flat-square)

</div>

**What this layer does:** This is the crown jewel of SIE v1.7. Any pre-1978 structure in Texas requires an asbestos abatement notification (TABS filing) with the Texas Department of Licensing & Regulation **before demolition can legally begin**. That filing names the General Contractor. L1A monitors TDLR for that filing — the moment it appears, the GC is identified and your contact target becomes crystal clear.

**What it found here:** The 1962 structure at 3100 & 3200 Hamilton Ave is definitively pre-1978. As of April 2, 2026, **no TABS filing has appeared in public search results and no GC has been named in any press coverage.** This means:

- Demo has not legally started yet, OR
- The asbestos survey is underway but the TABS filing hasn't been submitted, OR
- The GC has been selected internally but not publicly disclosed

**All three scenarios mean the sub-contract window is still open.**

**Weekly TDLR Search Protocol:**

```
URL: https://tdlr.texas.gov/tabs/search.htm

Search A → Address:  3100 Hamilton Ave, Fort Worth
Search B → Address:  3200 Hamilton Ave, Fort Worth
Search C → Owner:    Fort Worth Affordability
Search D → Developer: Ojala Partners
```

**Why it matters:** This layer exists because of a structural asymmetry in the demolition market. Most salvage operators don't know TABS filings exist. SIE treats them as the single most reliable GC identification signal available in the Texas market. When the filing appears, you have a 24–48 hour window to be first to the GC's phone.

### L2 — Freshness Scoring

<div align="center">

![Freshness](https://img.shields.io/badge/Freshness_Score-72_%2F_100-green?style=flat-square)

</div>

**What this layer does:** Freshness measures how close you are to the actual demolition event. A property with a 10-year-old foreclosure notice scores low. A property with a rezoning approval from 6 months ago and a 2026 groundbreaking target scores high. Freshness drives urgency calibration.

**What it found here:** Rezoning was approved October 2024. Phase 1 groundbreaking was targeted for 2026. You are running this analysis in April 2026 — the exact month Phase 1 was supposed to begin. Freshness score: **72/100.** This is a high-urgency property.

**Why it matters:** A score above 65 triggers an active monitoring cadence. Below 50, SIE recommends a quarterly check-in rather than weekly monitoring. At 72, the protocol is weekly TDLR checks, bi-weekly proxy signal scans, and an immediate outreach attempt to the developer.

### L3 — Seller Pressure Index (SPI)

<div align="center">

![SPI](https://img.shields.io/badge/SPI-LOW_—_Not_a_Distressed_Seller-lightgrey?style=flat-square)
![Driver](https://img.shields.io/badge/Urgency_Driver-Timeline_Pressure_(Not_Distress)-blue?style=flat-square)

</div>

**What this layer does:** SPI measures how motivated the property owner is to move quickly — typically driven by financial distress (tax delinquency, foreclosure, lis pendens), deteriorating condition, or ownership complexity. High SPI = owner eager to get someone in and get cash. Low SPI = owner is organized and in control.

**What it found here:** Fort Worth Housing Solutions is a mission-driven nonprofit with city backing and a structured development partner in Ojala Partners. This is not a distressed seller. SPI is low. **But timeline pressure substitutes for distress pressure here.** Phase 1 is supposed to open in 2026 — the developer is on the clock, and a delayed demo means a delayed opening.

**Why it matters:** Low SPI doesn't mean low urgency. It means your leverage isn't financial desperation — it's operational value. The pitch to Ojala and FWHS is not "I'll take this off your hands cheap." It's "I can accelerate your demo timeline and offset your costs." That reframe is critical.

### L4 — Structure Profile

<div align="center">

![Era](https://img.shields.io/badge/Era-1962_Garden--Style_Multifamily-blue?style=flat-square)
![Type](https://img.shields.io/badge/Type-Pre--1978_Wood_Frame_+_Masonry-orange?style=flat-square)
![Units](https://img.shields.io/badge/Units-430–484_Combined-informational?style=flat-square)
![Asbestos](https://img.shields.io/badge/Asbestos_Risk-HIGH_(Pre--1978)-red?style=flat-square)

</div>

**What this layer does:** Structure Profile determines what you're actually dealing with — construction type, era, size, and material composition. A 1970s commercial office building yields very differently from a 1940s warehouse or a 1990s wood-frame apartment complex. Era is the single biggest driver of copper and brass yield estimates.

**What it found here:** 1962 garden-style multifamily means:
- **High copper yield** — original plumbing was copper throughout; 1962 predates the PVC/CPVC transition
- **Brass fixtures** — original hardware is brass, not the cheaper alloys used post-1980
- **Moderate aluminum** — window frames and HVAC components
- **Lower structural steel** — garden-style wood-frame means less scrap metal per unit than commercial
- **Asbestos present** — pipe insulation, floor tile, roof materials all suspect in 1962 construction

**Why it matters:** The 1962 era is a sweet spot for salvage. Old enough to have original copper and brass throughout. New enough that the structure hasn't been picked clean by decades of renovation. This is one of the highest-yield residential structure profiles in the DFW market.

**What this layer does:** L5 estimates the gross material value (GMV) of salvageable materials based on structure era, unit count, and current DFW spot prices. It also applies the Prior Access Discount — a v1.6 addition — when tenants are still being relocated (elevated stripping risk).

**What it found here:**

| Material | Est. Yield | DFW Spot Range | Est. Gross Value |
|----------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Copper (HVAC, plumbing) | 8,000–12,000 lbs | $1.80–$3.20/lb | $14,400–$38,400 |
| Aluminum (windows, frames) | 15,000–25,000 lbs | $0.45–$0.70/lb | $6,750–$17,500 |
| Brass (fixtures, valves) | 2,000–4,000 lbs | $1.60–$2.20/lb | $3,200–$8,800 |

| Appliances (pre-tenant strip) | variable | $15–$40/unit | $3,000–$17,200 |

> ⚠️ **Prior Access Discount applied:** Tenants are being relocated in phases. Expect 15–25% reduction on copper and appliance line items due to tenant stripping risk before demo access is granted.

**Why it matters:** The GMV spread ($29,750–$91,500) is wide because two unknowns dominate: how much copper has already been stripped by relocating tenants, and whether appliances were removed before you get in. A site walk immediately closes this gap and would push YCS from 58 to 70+.

### L6 — Yield Confidence Score (YCS)

<div align="center">

![YCS](https://img.shields.io/badge/YCS-58_%2F_100-yellow?style=flat-square)
![Confidence](https://img.shields.io/badge/Confidence_Level-MODERATE-orange?style=flat-square)
![Trigger](https://img.shields.io/badge/Site_Walk_Would_Push_YCS_to_70%2B-blue?style=flat-square)

</div>

**What this layer does:** YCS is a confidence multiplier applied to L5's GMV estimates. A high YCS (80+) means you've had a site walk, confirmed structure integrity, and verified material presence. A low YCS means you're working from era-based assumptions only. YCS is multiplied against GMV before bid calculus runs.

**What it found here:** YCS of 58/100 reflects:
- ✅ 1962 era strongly suggests original copper and brass (positive)
- ✅ Unit count is confirmed (430–484 units is a large yield base)
- ⚠️ Tenant occupancy during phased relocation increases stripping risk (negative)
- ⚠️ No site walk has been completed — material condition is unverified
- ⚠️ Garden-style wood frame means less structural steel than initially estimated

**Why it matters:** YCS prevents overbidding on assumptions. A raw GMV of $91,500 sounds great. A YCS-adjusted GMV of $53,070 is what you actually put capital against. The spread closes the moment you get eyes on the property.

**What this layer does:** L7 takes the YCS-adjusted GMV, subtracts estimated costs (labor, haul, tools, disposal, margin buffer), and calculates a bid range: floor (60%), optimal (75%), and ceiling (88%). The APE-adjusted ceiling applies automatically when APE < 50, reducing the ceiling by 15% to protect you from overbidding on a low-access-probability deal.

**Bid Calculus (Phase 1 Only):**

| Metric | Low Scenario | High Scenario |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Adjusted GMV (YCS × Pre-YCS) | ~$17,255 | ~$53,070 |
| Labor + Haul + Disposal | -$8,000 | -$18,000 |
| **Net Value** | **~$9,255** | **~$35,070** |
| Bid Floor (60%) | $5,553 | $21,042 |
| Optimal Bid (75%) | $6,941 | $26,303 |
| Bid Ceiling (88%) | $8,144 | $30,862 |
| **APE-Adjusted Ceiling (×0.85)** | **$6,922** | **$26,233** |

> Phase 2 (~150+ additional units demolishing ~2029) represents a **separate, independent bid opportunity** with potentially higher APE if Phase 1 contact succeeds.

**Why it matters:** The bid calculus exists to prevent two failure modes: (1) leaving money on the table by bidding too low out of risk aversion, and (2) overbidding because the GMV number felt exciting before YCS and cost adjustments. The APE cascade is the safety rail that keeps you disciplined when access probability is low.

**What this layer does:** L8 splits competition into two tracks (bifurcated): institutional (large demo firms, national salvage companies) and street-level (local operators, scrappers, independent contractors). Each behaves differently and threatens your position in different ways.

**What it found here:**

- **Institutional:** Low threat. Large demo firms target the full demolition contract, not salvage sub-work on affordable housing redevelopments. They don't compete in this lane.
- **Street-level:** Active threat. The ZC-24-115 zoning case was a public record. Any Fort Worth operator monitoring Cultural District / West 7th development activity knows this project exists. The risk is not that they're smarter — it's that they may have already made the phone call.

**CPS Assessment:** Moderate-High. The 30-day action window is not a guideline — it is a hard deadline. Every week of inaction increases the probability that the GC sub-contract conversation has already happened with a competitor.

**Why it matters:** The bifurcated model prevents a common cognitive error — assuming your only competitors are large firms. In salvage, the most dangerous competitor is the operator two zip codes away who also reads public zoning notices and has a relationship with a local demolition GC.

**The Three-Step Play:**

```
Step 1 → Contact Fort Worth Housing Solutions + Ojala Partners NOW
```
